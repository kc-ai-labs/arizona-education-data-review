Assessment Predictions

Module 3 descriptive trend indicators for Math, ELA, and Science. Current forecasts are review prompts, not decision-ready model predictions.

Current assessment predictions are available at one grain: All Assessments, All Students, FAY.

Trend asks where this school is heading. Advanced asks how it is doing for the students it actually serves, and where its equity gaps are. Same school and subject; two different questions.

How to read this page (no statistics background needed)

FY2026 Indicator. The school's most recent actual score, carried forward. Before publishing, we tested fancier options by hiding FY2025 and asking each one to predict it from earlier years. On the current data run (February 2026 extracts), carrying the latest score forward won, so that is what we show. Honest and simple beats impressive and wrong.

The likely range. When we graded this method on FY2025, 8 of 10 predictions landed within the shaded band you see on the chart (as of the February 2026 run, about 7 points either way for larger schools and about 10 for small schools; each school's exact band is drawn on its chart). The range is measured from real errors, not assumed from a formula.

Annual Trend. A straight line through the school's FY2022 to FY2025 scores: how many points per year the school moved. It describes the past. Our testing showed it does not improve next-year predictions, which is why it is reported separately from the indicator.

FY2030 If-Trend-Continues. An illustration only: where the school would land if its recent yearly trend simply continued for five more years. No test can validate a projection that far out, so treat it as a conversation starter, not a forecast.

Confidence. Higher when we have all four recent years, the scores have been steady (3 points per year of movement or less), and enough students tested for percentages to be stable. Schools with fewer than 30 tested students never exceed "medium," because one student can move their percentage by several points.

Suppressed years and quality flags. The state redacts small-group results to protect privacy; those years never enter any calculation and are labeled in the table. Quality flags list data problems we found (for example, a school missing from the source extract). We show them rather than fix them silently, so a person can decide.

Full method, thresholds, and rationale: phase-03-predictive/README.md in the project repository. Every run's model comparison is stored in the database for audit.

How we trained this model

Training data. The model uses school-level Math, ELA, and Science assessment history at the same grain shown here: subject, test level, subgroup, and FAY status. Suppressed years are excluded from calculations and kept visible in the table.

  1. Set aside FY2025. The FY2025 holdout backtest fit FY2022 to FY2024, predicted FY2025, and graded each candidate against data it had not used for fitting.
  2. Compare candidates. The loader compares persistence, raw trend, and damped trend weights anchored to the latest actual score.
  3. Pick the honest winner. The selected weight comes from lowest holdout error, with ties favoring persistence. In the current run, w=0 won, so FY2026 carries forward the latest actual score.
  4. Calibrate the range. The likely range uses the 80th percentile of absolute FY2025 holdout errors, with separate widths for small and larger schools.
  5. Keep an audit trail. Each load stores source file hashes, run IDs, model metrics, quality flags, and forecast rows in MSSQL so the training path can be reviewed.

Training output remains a review prompt, not a causal claim or a decision-ready prediction.

How the likely range is calculated

It is not a standard deviation band. The shaded FY2026 range is calibrated from historical backtesting errors.

Backtest setup. FY2025 was treated as unknown, the model predicted FY2025 using FY2022 through FY2024, and the miss was measured in percentage points.

Band width. The displayed FY2026 range uses the 80th percentile of those absolute misses. In plain language, about 8 of 10 comparable backtested forecasts landed inside the band.

Small-school adjustment. Current calibration uses separate widths based on NumberTested: small schools use +/- 10.0 percentage points, and larger schools use +/- 7.0 percentage points.

What it does not mean. The band is a review aid, not a guarantee, not a standard statistical confidence interval, and not a causal claim. FY2030 has no band because it is an illustrative long-range continuation, not a calibrated one-year forecast.

How to read this (in plain English)

The idea. Schools serve very different students, so comparing them head-to-head is not fair. This view compares each school with other schools that serve similar students.

Performance vs. expectation. We estimate the score we would expect from a school based on who it teaches: how many students come from low-income homes, are still learning English, or receive special-education services, plus the school's size and mix of students. Then we compare that to the real score. Above the expected score is a bright spot worth learning from. Below is worth a closer look. The drivers list shows what pushes the expectation up or down; because these things overlap, read it as the general picture, not an exact split.

Equity gaps. The gap, in points, between groups of students at the same school (for example, students from low-income homes versus all students), and whether it has been getting smaller or larger in recent years. These are measured straight from the scores, not predicted. The gap versus a group's classmates is usually a little larger than shown, and gaps for very small groups are hidden because one or two students would swing them.

Use it honestly. This is a starting point for a conversation, not a grade or a ranking. Most of the expected score comes down to economics: schools serving more students in hardship tend to score lower, so being below expected is not a school's fault. Some schools, like online schools, do not fit the usual pattern, so always pair this with what you know. The numbers show patterns, never causes.

It holds up: tested on schools it had not seen, the expected score was about a third more accurate than guessing the average. Full method: phase-03-predictive/README.md.
